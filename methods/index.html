A study design is a tool for a question. This page explains the comparison logic and normalization problems behind the dossier.

Evidence signal

Strong causal evidence

Multiple RCTs, high-quality RCT/lottery evidence, or strong quasi-experimental evidence with clear student outcome effects.

SourceSourceSource

Promising causal/quasi

Credible lottery, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences, or well-controlled evidence, but narrower samples or mixed replication.

SourceSource

Mixed/conditional

Evidence varies by context, outcome, implementation, or student group; may include positive and null/negative findings.

Source

Limited/descriptive

Implementation, trend, or correlational evidence without strong causal identification.

SourceSource

Not an outcome intervention

A research infrastructure, standard, or framework that shapes evidence use but is not itself a student intervention.

SourceSource

Design glossary

RCT

Random assignment to treatment/control; strongest internal validity when implemented and analyzed well.

SourceSource

Lottery design

Uses oversubscribed admissions lotteries as random or near-random offers; estimates effects for applicants.

SourceSourceSource

Regression discontinuity

Compares students/schools just above and below an eligibility cutoff; local to the cutoff.

SourceSource

Difference-in-differences / event study

Compares pre-post changes for treated vs comparison groups; relies on parallel pre-trends.

SourceSource

Matched comparison / virtual control

Matches treated students/schools to similar untreated units; sensitive to unobserved selection.

SourceSource

Meta-analysis / systematic review

Aggregates effects across studies; results depend on inclusion rules and comparability.

SourceSourceSource

Normalization

Effect sizes

Convert outcome differences to standard deviations to compare tests and grades; interpret with outcome and age context.

Source

Common scales

Use low-stakes national or harmonized data such as NAEP or SEDA when state tests change.

SourceSource

ITT vs TOT

Intent-to-treat estimates effect of offer/assignment; treatment-on-treated estimates effect among compliers and needs stronger assumptions.

Source

Cost-effectiveness

Pair credible effect estimates with full implementation costs, not only grant totals or software licenses.

SourceSource

Input variable

Target population

Use baseline covariates, subgroup indicators, baseline achievement quantiles, and cohort definitions.

SourceSource

Dosage and intensity

Convert planned exposure to attended exposure; report ITT and treatment-on-treated where possible.

SourceSourceSource

Staffing ratio and adult contact

Normalize to effective contact hours and adult qualification level.

SourceSourceSource

Curriculum content

Use common rubrics, curriculum review ratings, lesson artifacts, topic coverage, and teacher usage surveys.

SourceSourceSource

Pedagogy and instructional model

Use validated observation rubrics, video coding, instructional logs, and common categories.

SourceSourceSource

Technology role

Separate access, active academic use, teacher-mediated use, and substitution for in-person instruction.

SourceSourceSource

Teacher supports

Report content focus, active practice, coaching cycles, and alignment to adopted materials.

SourceSource

Accountability and incentives

Code stakes, affected grades/subjects, thresholds, consequences, and timing.

SourceSourceSource

Governance and choice

Use lottery offers for ITT where possible; otherwise match on prior achievement and demographics.

SourceSourceSource

Time structure

Convert to active learning time using attendance and instructional-use measures.

SourceSource

Early childhood quality

Use classroom quality observations, curriculum indicators, and counterfactual care type.

SourceSourceSource

Whole-child supports

Track service eligibility, take-up, completion, and schoolwide climate exposure.

SourceSourceSource

Finance and resources

Use real dollars, exposure years, district fixed effects, and cost-effectiveness ratios.

SourceSource

Implementation and fidelity

Separate intent-to-treat, implementation-on-treated, and mediator analyses.

SourceSourceSource

Counterfactual context

Use baseline trends, matched controls, cohort fixed effects, and sensitivity analyses.

SourceSourceSource

Outcome

Achievement test scores

Convert to student-level or grade-level SD; use common NAEP/SEDA scales for cross-state comparisons.

SourceSourceSource

Proficiency and cut-score metrics

Do not equate state proficient with NAEP Proficient; map state standards carefully.

SourceSource

Reading subskills

Report domains separately; early decoding gains do not guarantee comprehension gains.

SourceSource

Math subskills and course readiness

Normalize by grade, course content, and test alignment.

SourceSource

Academic growth

Growth metrics are less sensitive to baseline level but still depend on test scale and ceiling/floor.

SourceSourceSource

Coursework and grades

Normalize course titles and rigor; credit inflation can mask learning.

SourceSourceSource

Attendance and engagement

Use same chronic absenteeism threshold and handle remote/partial days consistently.

SourceSource

Behavior and climate

Standardize reporting rules; separate actual behavior from adult referral behavior.

SourceSource

SEL and noncognitive outcomes

Use common scales or standardize within sample; triangulate self, teacher, and admin measures.

Source

Grade progression

Define on-track consistently; account for policy-induced retention.

SourceSource

Completion

Use consistent cohort graduation definitions and diploma requirements.

SourceSourceSource

Postsecondary outcomes

Compare enrollment quality, persistence, and degree, not only initial entry.

SourceSourceSource

Labor market and adult outcomes

Use real dollars and discounting; account for local labor markets and mobility.

SourceSourceSource

Health, crime, and civic outcomes

Use consistent legal/reporting definitions and subgroup precision checks.

SourceSource

Cost-effectiveness

Include start-up, training, administrative, facilities, opportunity costs, and participant time.

SourceSourceSource

Implementation mediators

Use as mechanism evidence, not final student outcome unless the theory is explicitly adult practice.
